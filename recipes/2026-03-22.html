Glycaemic Index
📊 Low GI

Oats (GI ~55), yoghurt (<20), banana (~51). The beta-glucan soluble fibre in oats slows glucose absorption significantly. Adding yoghurt further blunts the glycaemic response.

Health Benefits Addressed
📉
Lower LDL Cholesterol: The 9.5g of beta-glucan soluble fibre in 80g oats is clinically proven to reduce LDL cholesterol by binding bile acids in the gut. Just 3g/day is the EU-approved therapeutic dose — this breakfast provides 3× that.
❤️
Cardiovascular Health: Potassium (1,038mg across all sources) supports healthy blood pressure. Magnesium (145mg, 35% RDI) is critical for cardiac muscle function and vasodilation.
🩸
Blood Pressure: High potassium : sodium ratio (1038mg K vs ~110mg Na) is strongly associated with lower blood pressure. Oats have near-zero sodium (2mg/100g).
🦠
Gut Health: Natural yoghurt with live cultures provides probiotics (Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium). Oat beta-glucan acts as a prebiotic, feeding beneficial gut bacteria.
Cooking Instructions
1
Add 80g rolled oats and 100mL milk to a small saucepan. Add 150mL water. Stir to combine.
2
Heat over medium heat, stirring occasionally, for 4–5 minutes until thickened to your preferred consistency. Season with a pinch of cinnamon if desired.
3
Pour into a bowl. Slice the banana over the top.
4
Spoon yoghurt alongside or on top. Serve immediately.

⚠️ Iron from lentils is non-haem iron (plant-based). The vitamin C from tomatoes, peas and onion in this meal enhances absorption by 2–4×. Bioavailable iron is estimated at ~25–35% of stated value.

Glycaemic Index
📊 Low GI

Red lentils have a GI of ~21–26 — one of the lowest of any food. Combined with high fibre (30.6g) and protein (32.8g), this meal produces an exceptionally blunted blood glucose response.

Health Benefits Addressed
🛡️
Anti-Cancer: Lycopene from cooked tomatoes (bioavailability increases 3–4× when heated with fat) is associated with reduced prostate and breast cancer risk. Allicin from garlic and quercetin from onion have demonstrated anti-tumour properties in multiple studies.
📉
Lower LDL Cholesterol: Soluble fibre from lentils (13.7g/100g dry) and peas lowers LDL by binding bile acids. Lentils also contain plant sterols that compete with dietary cholesterol for absorption.
❤️
Cardiovascular Health: 1,858mg of potassium — 49% of daily RDI in one meal — is strongly associated with reduced stroke and heart disease risk. Folate (266µg, 67% RDI) helps reduce homocysteine levels, a cardiovascular risk marker.
🧬
Beta-Carotene Powerhouse: 150g of carrot provides 11,733µg beta-carotene, converted to vitamin A as needed. Cooked with olive oil, bioavailability increases significantly. Vitamin A supports immune function, skin health, and eye health.
Cooking Instructions
1
Rinse 100g red lentils under cold water until the water runs clear. Set aside.
2
Heat olive oil in a medium saucepan over medium heat. Add diced onion and cook 3 minutes until softened. Add crushed garlic, cumin, and turmeric; stir for 30 seconds.
3
Add lentils, diced carrot, canned tomatoes, and 400mL water. Stir well. Bring to a boil, then reduce heat and simmer 15–18 minutes, stirring occasionally, until lentils are soft.
4
Add frozen peas in the final 3 minutes of cooking. Season with a pinch of salt and black pepper. Serve hot.

✅ Canned salmon with soft bones provides 191mg calcium per 100g — comparable to dairy. The vitamin D in this meal exceeds the weekly requirement of many adults in a single serving.

Glycaemic Index
📊 Low–Medium GI

Brown rice GI ~55 (vs ~72 for white rice). Broccoli and peas have GI <20. The combination of protein (38.6g), fat (17.4g), and fibre (17.9g) significantly slows glucose absorption, making the actual glycaemic response lower than brown rice alone.

Health Benefits Addressed
❤️
Cardiovascular Health: 1,576mg of long-chain omega-3 fatty acids (EPA+DHA) — 258% of RDI — reduces triglycerides, prevents arrhythmias, and reduces inflammation. This alone meets the heart-health omega-3 target for the entire week if consumed daily.
🛡️
Anti-Cancer: Broccoli contains sulforaphane — one of the most potent anti-cancer compounds known. It activates Nrf2 pathways, upregulating detoxification enzymes. 200g provides a clinically relevant dose. Garlic adds allicin (antibacterial, anti-tumour). Selenium (64% RDI) is a critical antioxidant co-factor.
☀️
Vitamin D — a rare budget win: Most Australians are deficient despite sun exposure. At 19.6µg per serving (392% of RDI), this single meal restores vitamin D stores. Vitamin D is critical for immune function, bone density, and mood regulation. No other budget food source comes close — canned salmon is exceptional.
🦴
Bone Health: Calcium from soft salmon bones (181mg) + broccoli (72mg) + peas = 309mg (31% RDI). Combined with 392% of RDI for vitamin D (which is required for calcium absorption), this meal delivers exceptional bone-building benefit.
Cooking Instructions
1
Rinse 80g brown rice. Cook in a small pot with 200mL water: bring to boil, cover, reduce to lowest heat for 25 minutes. Remove from heat, rest covered for 5 minutes.
2
While rice cooks, cut broccoli into florets and carrot into batons. Steam or boil for 6–7 minutes until just tender but still vibrant. Add frozen peas in the last 2 minutes.
3
Drain canned salmon. Open, tip into a small bowl, and flake with a fork. Don't discard the soft bones — mash them in for maximum calcium. Drizzle with soy sauce.
4
Heat olive oil in a small pan over medium heat. Add minced garlic and stir for 30 seconds until fragrant (do not brown).
5
Assemble: rice in bowl, top with steamed vegetables, then salmon. Drizzle garlic oil over the top. Serve immediately.
